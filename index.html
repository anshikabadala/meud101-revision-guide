Other Engineering Curves:
  • Cycloid: Point on circle rolling on a STRAIGHT LINE. Used in gear teeth.
  • Epicycloid: Point on circle rolling OUTSIDE another circle.
  • Hypocycloid: Point on circle rolling INSIDE another circle.
  • Involute: End of thread being UNWOUND from a circle/polygon. Used in gear tooth profiles (most common gear type).
  • Archimedean Spiral: Point moving away from centre at uniform rate while rotating uniformly. Equal distance between turns. Used in watch springs, scroll compressors.
  • Helix: Point on surface of cylinder rotating + moving axially. Like a screw thread or spring. Used in bolts, springs, helical gears.
Ellipse by Concentric Circle Method (5 marks):
  1. Draw centre O. Mark major axis AB = 100mm, minor axis CD = 60mm (assume these if not given).
  2. Draw two concentric circles: outer (radius = 50mm = semi-major), inner (radius = 30mm = semi-minor).
  3. Divide BOTH circles into 12 equal parts (30° each). Draw all 12 radii through both circles.
  4. At each point on OUTER circle → draw VERTICAL line (parallel to minor axis CD).
  5. At corresponding point on INNER circle → draw HORIZONTAL line (parallel to major axis AB).
  6. Intersection of these two lines = one point on ellipse. Mark all 12 points.
  7. Join all points + A, B, C, D smoothly = ELLIPSE.
A, B, C, D lie directly on the ellipse. Draw construction lines light (H pencil), final curve dark (HB pencil). Always state assumed dimensions.

5 marksMCQ

Isometric Projection = Single view showing 3 dimensions (L, W, H) simultaneously. All 3 axes equally inclined to picture plane.

Key Terms:
  • Isometric Axes: One vertical + two at 30° to horizontal. THREE AXES AT 120° TO EACH OTHER.
  • Isometric Lines: Lines parallel to any isometric axis. Can be measured directly.
  • Non-Isometric Lines: NOT parallel to any axis (inclined surfaces). Cannot measure directly.

Isometric Scale

Isometric scale factor = cos45° / cos30° = 0.7071 / 0.8660 = 0.816

Isometric VIEW (exam) = use ACTUAL dimensions directly

Isometric PROJECTION = multiply by 0.816

Box Method to draw isometric view from FV + TV:
  1. Find overall L × W × H from FV and TV
  2. Draw isometric axes (1 vertical, 2 at 30°)
  3. Draw overall bounding isometric box
  4. Mark all features on box faces by transferring dimensions
  5. Cut away parts that don't belong
  6. Draw visible edges dark. NO hidden lines in isometric.
  7. Erase all construction lines
Circles in isometric = ELLIPSES. Draw them using the 4-centre oval method. Never draw actual circles in isometric views.

ZEROTH LAW: "If A is in thermal equilibrium with C, and B is in thermal equilibrium with C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium." → Basis of thermometry. Allows temperature measurement.

FIRST LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS: "Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only converted from one form to another."

First Law

Q = W + ΔU

Q = heat added, W = work done, ΔU = change in internal energy

BOYLE'S LAW (constant T): Volume ∝ 1/Pressure — doubling pressure halves volume.

Boyle's Law

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (temperature constant)

CHARLES'S LAW (constant P): Volume ∝ Temperature — heating gas expands it.

Charles's Law

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (pressure constant) — T MUST be in KELVIN

Example: V₁=2m³, T₁=300K, T₂=600K → V₂ = 2×600/300 = 4m³

COMBINED GAS LAW:

Combined

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

CHARACTERISTIC EQUATION (Perfect Gas):

Perfect Gas

PV = mRT

P=pressure(Pa), V=volume(m³), m=mass(kg), R=gas constant(J/kg·K), T=Kelvin

For Air: R = 0.287 kJ/kg·K

3 marks 2025MCQ 2025

1. REFRIGERANT: Heat-carrying working fluid circulating through refrigeration system. Absorbs heat from refrigerated space (in evaporator), rejects it to surroundings (in condenser). Properties: non-toxic, non-flammable, high latent heat, low cost. Examples: R-12, R-22, R-134a, NH₃ (Ammonia).

2. COEFFICIENT OF PERFORMANCE (COP):

COP Formula

COP = Refrigerating Effect / Work Input = Q₂ / W = Q₂ / (Q₁ - Q₂)

Carnot COP (ideal) = T₂ / (T₁ - T₂) [T in Kelvin]

Higher COP = more efficient (cools more with less electricity)

3. REFRIGERATING EFFECT: Total heat absorbed from refrigerated space per kg of refrigerant during one cycle. Happens in EVAPORATOR. Unit: kJ/kg or kJ/min. Also called "cooling effect." 1 TR = 3.5 kW = 210 kJ/min.

ComponentInlet stateWhat it doesOutlet state
1. CompressorLow P, Low T vapourCompresses refrigerant (uses electricity)High P, High T vapour
2. CondenserHigh P, High T vapourRejects heat to atmosphere → condensesHigh P, High T liquid

Surveying — Definition, Principle (Whole to Part), Classification, Tapes"Explain principle of whole to part" — 4 marks 2025
4 marks 2025MCQ
▾
Surveying = Science and art of measuring Earth's surface — distances, angles, elevations — to determine relative positions and prepare maps/plans.

Objects: Prepare maps and plans; fix land boundaries; provide data for construction; calculate areas/volumes.

Primary Divisions:
  • Plane Surveying — Earth treated as flat. For areas <250 sq km. Curvature ignored. Used for most engineering projects.
  • Geodetic Surveying — Takes Earth's curved shape into account. For large areas (countries, continents). National mapping.
PRINCIPLE: Work from WHOLE to PART

Problem with "Part to Whole": Every measurement has a small error. As you add section after section, errors ACCUMULATE. The final result has large compounded error — boundaries may not close.

Solution — "Whole to Part":
  1. First establish a few CONTROL POINTS spread across the ENTIRE area (measured with high precision)
  2. These form the overall framework/skeleton
  3. Fill in details between control points using ordinary methods
  4. Any errors in detail work stay LOCAL — don't spread throughout
  5. Multiple teams can work simultaneously on different sections
Benefits: Errors localized, not accumulated. Multiple teams can work simultaneously. Mistakes easily detected. Overall accuracy maintained even with local errors.
Analogy: Drawing India's map — first mark 4 corners accurately (whole), then states, then districts, then cities (parts). Not the other way around!

What are engineering curves? Name them all.Your friend was asked this!
Very likely
▾
Engineering curves are special mathematical curves used in engineering design and drawing.

1. Conic Sections — curves obtained by cutting a cone at different angles: Circle, Ellipse, Parabola, Hyperbola.

2. Cycloid — path traced by a point on the circumference of a circle rolling along a STRAIGHT LINE without slipping. Like tracking a point on a rolling wheel. Used in gear tooth profiles.

3. Epicycloid — circle rolls OUTSIDE another fixed circle. Used in epicyclic gears.

4. Hypocycloid — circle rolls INSIDE another fixed circle.

5. Involute — curve traced by the end of a thread as it is UNWOUND from a circle. Like unrolling a spool of thread. Used in gear tooth design (most common gear tooth shape).

6. Archimedean Spiral — point moves away from a fixed point at uniform rate while also rotating uniformly. Turns are equally spaced. Used in watch springs, cam mechanisms.

7. Helix — point moves along a cylinder while rotating around it. Like a screw thread or spring. Used in bolts, springs, helical gears.

What is orthographic projection? Explain first and third angle.Most important drawing VIVA question
Very likely
▾
Orthographic Projection = A method of drawing a 3D object on paper by projecting it onto 2D flat planes from different directions. The projecting lines (called projectors) are parallel to each other and perpendicular to the plane. You get multiple views — Front, Top, Side — that together fully describe the object.

Why orthographic? Unlike a photo, orthographic views show TRUE shapes and TRUE dimensions — essential for manufacturing.

Three principal planes:
  • VP (Vertical Plane) → Front View
  • HP (Horizontal Plane) → Top View
  • PP (Profile Plane) → Side View
First Angle Projection (India/Europe): Object is placed between the observer and the planes. The view "falls through" and lands on the plane behind/below.
  • Top View goes BELOW Front View
  • Left Side View goes on RIGHT side
  • Right Side View goes on LEFT side
Third Angle Projection (USA): Planes are between the observer and object. Like looking through glass.
  • Top View goes ABOVE Front View
  • Right Side View goes on RIGHT side
India uses FIRST ANGLE. Simple rule: In first angle, the view goes to the OPPOSITE side from where you're looking.

What is a sectional view? Why is it used? Types?Your friend was asked "where will sectional views be cut down"
Very likely — friend was asked!
▾
Sectional View = An imaginary cut is made through an object with a cutting plane, one part is removed, and you look at the exposed internal features. It reveals the INSIDE of the object clearly.

Why used? When an object has many internal features (holes, slots, cavities), using hidden lines makes the drawing very confusing. A section view removes that confusion by showing inside directly.

Types of sectional views:
  • Full Section — cutting plane cuts completely through. Entire view shows cross-section.
  • Half Section — only one quarter removed. Half shows inside, half shows outside. Used for SYMMETRICAL objects.
  • Offset Section — cutting plane is stepped (offset) to pass through features not in one straight line.
  • Revolved Section — cross-section is rotated 90° in place on the view itself.
  • Partial (Broken-out) Section — only small portion cut open.
Hatching (Section lines): Thin lines at 45°, equally spaced (2–5mm). Adjacent parts have opposite direction or different spacing. Do NOT hatch bolts, nuts, solid shafts, ribs — even when cut.

Isometric Projection = A type of 3D drawing where all three axes (length, width, height) are equally inclined to the picture plane and equally foreshortened. Unlike orthographic (which needs multiple views), isometric shows all 3 dimensions in ONE single view.

Isometric Axes:
  • Three axes — one VERTICAL, and two at 30° to horizontal (one going left, one going right)
  • All three axes are at 120° to each other

What is an ellipse? How do you draw it by concentric circle method?You may draw an ellipse in practical
Practical topic
▾
Ellipse = A closed oval curve. It has two axes — Major axis (longer) and Minor axis (shorter) — both passing through the centre and perpendicular to each other. It is a conic section where eccentricity e is between 0 and 1.

Concentric Circle Method — Steps:
  1. Draw horizontal major axis AB and vertical minor axis CD through centre O.
  2. Draw two concentric circles with centre O — outer circle (radius = semi-major axis) and inner circle (radius = semi-minor axis).
  3. Divide both circles into 12 equal parts (every 30°). Draw radii through both circles.
  4. At each point on OUTER circle → draw a vertical line (parallel to minor axis).
  5. At corresponding point on INNER circle → draw a horizontal line (parallel to major axis).
  6. Where these two lines meet = one point on the ellipse. Mark it.
  7. Repeat for all 12 points. Join all points + A, B, C, D with a smooth curve.
Logic: Outer circle gives Y-coordinate, inner circle gives X-coordinate. Their intersection is the ellipse point. This works because the ellipse equation is x²/a² + y²/b² = 1.

What is Charles's Law? State it with formula.2-mark exam question — also VIVA
2 marks exam + VIVA
▾
Charles's Law states: "At constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of a perfect gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature."

Simply: If you heat a gas (keeping pressure same), it expands. If you cool it, it shrinks.

Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (Temperature MUST be in Kelvin — K = °C + 273)
Where V = volume, T = absolute temperature
Real-life example: A balloon expands in hot sun and shrinks in cold weather — same pressure (atmosphere), but temperature changes volume.

Boyle's Law (just to know the difference): At constant TEMPERATURE, P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (pressure and volume are inversely proportional).
14
What is the First Law of Thermodynamics?
▾
First Law of Thermodynamics: "Energy can neither be created nor destroyed — it can only be converted from one form to another."

This is the law of Conservation of Energy.

Formula: Q = W + ΔU
Q = heat added to the system
W = work done BY the system
ΔU = increase in internal energy of the system
Example: In a petrol engine — chemical energy of fuel → heat energy (combustion) → mechanical energy (piston moves) → kinetic energy (car moves). Total energy is conserved throughout.

What are the types of steam? What is dryness fraction?
▾
Types of steam:

1. Wet Steam — mixture of steam + water droplets. Temperature = saturation temperature. Dryness fraction x is between 0 and 1. Not suitable for turbines (water damages blades).

2. Dry Saturated Steam — all water has converted to steam. Temperature = saturation temperature (100°C at 1 atm). Dryness fraction x = 1 exactly.

3. Superheated Steam — temperature is ABOVE saturation temperature. Behaves like a perfect gas (obeys Boyle's and Charles's laws). Best for turbines — more energy, no water droplets.

Dryness Fraction (x) = mass of dry steam / total mass of wet steam mixture.
  • x = 0 → all water (just saturated water, no steam)
  • x = 1 → dry saturated steam (all steam, no water)
  • 0 < x < 1 → wet steam (mixture)

Describe the Cochran Boiler.Most popular boiler in VIVA

▾
Cochran Boiler = A vertical, multi-tube, internally fired, fire-tube, natural circulation boiler.

Working:

What is TDC, BDC, stroke, bore, compression ratio?Basic terminology VIVA question
▾
TDC (Top Dead Centre) = Highest position the piston can reach in the cylinder.
BDC (Bottom Dead Centre) = Lowest position the piston can reach.
Stroke = Distance piston travels from TDC to BDC (or BDC to TDC). One-way travel.
Bore = Internal diameter of the cylinder.
Swept Volume (Vs) = Volume swept by piston from TDC to BDC = (π/4) × D² × L
Clearance Volume (Vc) = Small volume remaining above piston when it's at TDC. Cannot be zero.
Compression Ratio (r) = (Swept Volume + Clearance Volume) / Clearance Volume
= (Vs + Vc) / Vc
SI engines: r = 6:1 to 12:1 | CI engines: r = 14:1 to 22:1

4-Stroke Engine: The complete working cycle takes FOUR strokes (movements) of the piston = 2 complete rotations of crankshaft. One power stroke per 2 revolutions.

4 strokes are:

Refrigeration and Air Conditioning
25
What is refrigeration? What is its principle?Your friend was asked about refrigerator — start here
Very likely — friend was asked!
▾
Refrigeration = The process of removing heat from a body/space at a temperature LOWER than the surrounding atmosphere and maintaining that lower temperature by continuously extracting heat — against the natural direction of heat flow — by supplying external work (electricity).

Principle: Heat naturally flows from HOT to COLD on its own (Second Law of Thermodynamics). In refrigeration, we want the OPPOSITE — move heat from COLD space (inside fridge) to HOT surroundings (room). This does NOT happen on its own. So we supply EXTERNAL WORK (electricity to run the compressor) to force this to happen.

Applications: Food preservation, medicines, vaccines, ice making, air conditioning, chemical processing, blood banks, server cooling.
26
What are the 4 main parts of a refrigerator (VCR cycle)? Explain each.
▾
VCR = Vapour Compression Refrigeration — used in home fridge, car AC, commercial refrigerators.

4 main parts:

1. Compressor — "The heart of the system." Takes low-pressure, low-temperature refrigerant gas → compresses it → becomes HIGH pressure, HIGH temperature gas. Powered by electric motor.

2. Condenser — High pressure hot gas enters → rejects heat to surrounding air → becomes HIGH pressure LIQUID. (The hot coils at the back of your fridge is the condenser).

3. Expansion Valve — High pressure liquid passes through a tiny opening → pressure drops suddenly → temperature drops dramatically → LOW pressure, LOW temperature liquid+vapour mixture.

4. Evaporator — The cold refrigerant enters → ABSORBS heat from the food/room inside → evaporates and becomes LOW pressure gas → back to compressor. THIS IS WHERE COOLING HAPPENS.

Cycle: Compressor → Condenser → Expansion Valve → Evaporator → back to Compressor
The COMPRESSOR does NOT cool. The EVAPORATOR produces the cooling effect by absorbing heat from the refrigerated space.

What is a refrigerant? What properties should a good refrigerant have?3-mark exam question + VIVA
3 marks + VIVA
▾
Refrigerant = The working fluid that circulates through the refrigeration system. It absorbs heat from the cold space (in the evaporator) and carries it to the condenser where it is rejected to the atmosphere.

Properties of a GOOD refrigerant:
  • Non-toxic (safe for humans)
  • Non-flammable (safe near heat sources)
  • Non-corrosive (does not damage pipes and components)
  • High latent heat of vaporization (absorbs more heat per kg)
  • Low specific volume (smaller compressor needed)
  • Low boiling point (evaporates easily at low temperatures)
  • Cheap and easily available
  • Eco-friendly (does not damage ozone layer)
Common refrigerants: R-12 (Freon — being phased out, harms ozone), R-22 (AC use), R-134a (modern eco-friendly), NH₃ or Ammonia (industrial, high efficiency but toxic), CO₂ (marine refrigeration).

What is COP? What is refrigerating effect? What is 1 TR?
▾
COP (Coefficient of Performance) = Measure of efficiency of a refrigeration system. Ratio of useful cooling produced to electrical work input required.
COP = Refrigerating Effect / Work Input = Q₂ / W = Q₂ / (Q₁ - Q₂)
Higher COP = more efficient (cools more, uses less electricity)
Refrigerating Effect = The actual amount of heat absorbed by the refrigerant from the refrigerated space (food, room) during one complete cycle. This is the cooling produced. Unit: kJ/kg or kJ/min. It happens in the EVAPORATOR.

1 Tonne of Refrigeration (TR) = The refrigerating capacity needed to melt 1000 kg (1 tonne) of ice at 0°C in 24 hours.
1 TR = 3.5 kW = 3.5 kJ/s = 210 kJ/min
When you see "1.5-ton AC" or "2-ton fridge" — they mean 1.5 TR and 2 TR cooling capacity.

What is surveying? What is the principle of "whole to part"?Your friend was asked about surveying — 4-mark exam question too
Very likely — friend was asked!
▾
Surveying = The science and art of measuring Earth's surface — distances, angles, elevations — to determine the relative position of points and represent them on a map or plan.

Objects of surveying: Prepare maps; fix land boundaries; plan roads/dams/buildings; calculate areas and volumes.

Principle — Work from WHOLE to PART:

Problem with "Part to Whole": If you measure small sections and try to join them, every small measurement has a tiny error. All those errors ADD TOGETHER (accumulate). By the time you finish, the boundary doesn't close — massive total error.

Solution — "Whole to Part":
  1. First measure a few widely-spaced CONTROL POINTS covering the ENTIRE area with high precision
  2. These form the overall framework (skeleton) of the survey
  3. Then fill in details between control points with ordinary methods
  4. Any error in detail work stays LOCAL — does NOT spread to the whole survey
Benefits: Errors stay contained, multiple teams can work simultaneously, mistakes easy to find and fix, overall accuracy maintained.
Real-life analogy: Making India's map — first mark the 4 corners accurately (whole), then add states, then districts, then cities (parts). Never the other way around.

What is plane surveying vs geodetic surveying?
▾
Plane Surveying: The Earth's surface is treated as a FLAT (plane) surface. Curvature of Earth is ignored. Used for small areas — less than 250 square km. Used for most engineering projects (roads, buildings, dams).

Geodetic Surveying: Takes the Earth's CURVED shape into account. Used for very large areas — surveying whole countries, continents. Used for national mapping, GPS systems, satellite-based measurements.
MCQ/VIVA: Ignores Earth's curvature → Plane Surveying. Considers Earth's curvature → Geodetic Surveying.

Invar Tape = A measuring tape used in precise surveying. Made of an alloy of 36% Nickel + 64% Steel.

Why Invar? Normal steel expands when hot and contracts when cold (thermal expansion). In surveying, even 1mm error multiplied over large distances causes big mistakes. Other tape types for comparison:

Foundation = The lowest part of a building structure, constructed below ground level. Its purpose is to transfer ALL the loads of the building safely to the soil below without excessive settlement or failure.

Why is foundation important? Without a proper foundation, the building would sink, tilt or collapse. The foundation distributes the heavy concentrated load of walls and columns over a larger area of soil.

Two main types:

  • Combined footing — supports two or more columns
  • Raft/Mat footing — single slab covers entire building area; used on WEAK soil
Deep Foundation (depth > width) — when hard soil is very deep:
  • Pile foundation — long cylindrical columns driven deep into ground until they reach hard rock. Used for bridges, tall buildings, waterlogged soil.

What are the types of loads on a building? Explain each.4-mark exam question + VIVA
4 marks + VIVA
▾
1. Dead Load (DL) = Permanent weight of the building's own parts — walls, columns, beams, slabs, permanent fixtures. Never changes. Always acts downward (gravity).

2. Live Load (LL) = Movable, temporary loads that can change — people, furniture, equipment, vehicles. Also called "imposed load." Acts downward.

3. Wind Load (WL) = Pressure exerted by wind on building surfaces. Acts HORIZONTALLY (sideways). Important for tall buildings. Formula: P = K × V² (V = wind speed). Can topple or overturn a building if not designed for it.

4. Earthquake Load (EL) = Horizontal force caused by ground shaking during earthquakes. Pushes the building sideways like a pendulum. Formula: F = W × α/g. Gujarat is in Seismic Zone III — high earthquake risk.

5. Snow Load = Weight of accumulated snow on roof. Relevant in cold regions (Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh).

Key rule: Dead + Live loads are VERTICAL (pull down). Wind + Earthquake loads are HORIZONTAL (push sideways). A building must resist BOTH types safely.

What is a lintel? What is a chajja? What is a plinth?Building components — very commonly asked
Very likely
▾
Lintel = A horizontal beam (usually RCC or stone) provided ABOVE a door or window opening in a wall. Its job is to take the weight/load of the wall above the opening and transfer it to the sides. Without a lintel, the wall above the opening would collapse. Height from floor = 2.0m. Must extend 10cm on each side of opening.

Chajja (Sun shade / Weather shed) = A horizontal slab that projects outward from the wall just above doors and windows. It protects the opening from rain (prevents water entering) and sun (reduces direct sunlight). Projection = 45–90cm.

Plinth = The portion of the building structure between ground level and the finished floor level of the ground floor. Height = 30–90cm. Protects the building from moisture rising from ground, insects, and splashing rainwater. The DPC (Damp Proof Course) is placed at plinth level.

Sill = Horizontal member BELOW a window opening. Supports the window frame. Height from floor = 70–90cm.

Parapet = A low wall (1.0–1.3m high) at the edge of a roof or terrace. Prevents people from falling off. Min thickness = 10cm.

What is DPC? What is its purpose?
▾
DPC = Damp Proof Course = A horizontal layer of waterproof material placed in a wall at plinth level to prevent moisture from rising up through the walls from the ground.

Why needed? Soil is always moist. Bricks and mortar absorb water through capillary action (like a sponge). Without DPC, moisture rises up the walls → peeling paint, crumbling plaster, unhealthy damp rooms, damaged furniture.

Materials used for DPC: Bitumen (most common), 1:1 cement-sand mortar, waterproof concrete, lead sheets, polythene sheets.

Typical thickness: 2.5cm to 4cm.

If you draw a CYLINDER — isometric view. How does a circle appear in isometric?Your friend drew a cylinder!

▾
Isometric View of a Cylinder:

In isometric view, a cylinder appears as a rectangular box-like shape with ELLIPSES on the top and bottom faces, and two tangent lines connecting them on the sides.

How a circle becomes an ellipse in isometric: In isometric, the three planes are tilted. Any circle lying in an isometric plane gets "squashed" and appears as an ellipse. This is because you're viewing the circular face at an angle, not straight on.

How to draw the ellipse (4-centre oval method):
  1. Draw the isometric square for that face
  2. Find the midpoint of each of the 4 sides
  3. Draw perpendiculars at each midpoint — these intersect at 4 centres
  4. Draw 4 arcs using these 4 centres — this approximate ellipse represents the circle
VIVA questions about cylinder drawing:
  • "Why is the circle drawn as an ellipse?" → Because in isometric projection, circular faces appear as ellipses due to the inclined viewing angle
  • "How many isometric axes are there?" → Three — one vertical and two at 30° to horizontal, 120° to each other
  • "What is the isometric scale factor?" → 0.816

Q: What projection method have you used?
A: First Angle Projection (as per BIS/Indian standard SP:46).

Q: Why is the top view placed below the front view?
A: In first angle projection, the object is between the observer and the projection plane. The top view "shadow" falls on the horizontal plane which unfolds downward, so it appears below the front view.

Q: What do the different types of lines in your drawing mean?
A: Thick continuous lines = visible edges. Dashed lines = hidden/invisible edges. Chain lines (long-short) = centre lines showing axis of symmetry. Thin lines with arrows = dimension lines.

Q: What is a hidden line and why do we use it?
A: A hidden line (shown as dashed) represents an edge or feature that exists in the object but is not visible from the viewing direction in that particular view. It helps complete the description without needing an extra view.

Q: What are the three views in orthographic projection?
A: Front View (FV) on VP, Top View (TV) on HP, Side View (SV) on PP.

Q: What is a cutting plane? What is hatching?
A: Cutting plane is the imaginary plane that cuts through the object in a sectional view. Hatching (section lines) are thin parallel lines at 45° that fill the cut surface to show the material has been cut.

Q: What scale did you use?
A: [Say whatever scale you used, e.g.] "I used a 1:1 (full size) scale" or "I used a 1:2 reducing scale." Then: Scale means the ratio of drawing size to actual size.

Q: What is the difference between isometric view and orthographic view?
A: Orthographic projection shows multiple 2D views (front, top, side) separately — each shows true shape and size of one face. Isometric view shows all three dimensions in ONE pictorial view — it gives a 3D appearance but does not show true dimensions of inclined surfaces.

Q: What is the difference between a plan and a map?
A: A plan shows a SMALL area at LARGE scale (like a building floor plan — 1:100). A map shows a LARGE area at SMALL scale (like a country map — 1:50000). Plans have very detailed information, maps show general features.

Q: Why do we not show hidden lines in isometric views?
A: Isometric is a PICTORIAL view — it's meant to look like a 3D photograph of the object. Hidden lines make it cluttered and hard to read without adding useful information. They are omitted unless specifically needed to show a critical feature.

Q: What happens when you heat steam beyond dry saturated — what do you call it?
A: It becomes SUPERHEATED STEAM. The temperature rises above the saturation temperature, steam starts behaving like a perfect gas (obeys Boyle's and Charles's laws), and it has more energy. It is preferred in steam turbines.

Q: Why does a CI engine have higher compression ratio than SI engine?
A: In CI (diesel) engine, there is no spark plug. The diesel is ignited by the HEAT of compressed air. Air must reach about 600°C — which requires very high compression (14–22:1). In SI (petrol) engine, a spark plug ignites the mixture, so high compression is not needed. Also, petrol would pre-ignite (knock) at high compression.

Q: What is eccentricity? For which curve is it equal to 1?
A: Eccentricity (e) = ratio of distance from focus to distance from directrix. It defines the shape of conic sections. e=0 (circle), 0<e<1 (ellipse), e=1 (PARABOLA), e>1 (hyperbola).

Q: What is the involute of a circle used for?
A: Gear teeth profiles. The involute curve ensures that gear teeth mesh smoothly with constant velocity ratio, low friction, and can work even with small variations in centre distance. Almost all modern gears use involute tooth profiles.

Q: If the cutting plane is inclined to HP, is apparent section equal to true section?
A: NO. When the cutting plane is INCLINED to HP, the apparent section (AS) is LESS than the true section (TS). The section appears compressed/distorted. To find the true section, you need to project onto an Auxiliary Inclined Plane (AIP) that is parallel to the cutting plane.
